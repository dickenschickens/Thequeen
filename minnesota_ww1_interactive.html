What caused World War I?
Click each cause to learn more — there were 4 big reasons!

World War I didn't start from just one thing. Historians remember the causes with the word MAIN — Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, and Nationalism. Click each card to explore!

⚔️
M — Militarism

Armies & Arms Race

European countries were building up huge armies and navies, especially Germany and Britain. Countries thought that having the biggest military would keep them safe — but it actually made war more likely because everyone was ready to fight!
🤝
A — Alliances

Secret Agreements

Countries made secret promises to protect each other. Europe split into two groups: the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain). When one country went to war, all its allies had to join — like dominoes falling!
🌍
I — Imperialism

Competition for Colonies

European countries competed to control land in Africa and Asia. This competition created jealousy and conflict. Germany felt it deserved more colonies than it had, which created tension with Britain and France.
🏳️
N — Nationalism

Pride & Independence

People in different regions wanted their own countries and felt very proud of their heritage. In Austria-Hungary, groups like Serbians wanted independence. This nationalism created conflict, especially in the Balkans region of Europe.

🔫 The Spark: Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand

On June 28, 1914, Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary was shot and killed in Sarajevo by a Serbian nationalist named Gavrilo Princip. This single event triggered the alliance system — within weeks, nearly all of Europe was at war! World War I officially began in August 1914.

Why did the United States join the war?
The US waited 3 years — here's why it finally jumped in

🌊 At First: Staying Out

When war broke out in 1914, President Woodrow Wilson declared that the US would stay neutral — meaning the US wouldn't pick a side. Many Americans didn't want to get involved in Europe's problems. This was called isolationism.

Flip each card to see the reasons the US changed its mind:

🚢 The Lusitania Sinking (1915) — tap to reveal
A German submarine sank the British ship Lusitania. 1,198 people died — including 128 Americans! This outraged the US public and turned many Americans against Germany.
📜 The Zimmermann Telegram (1917) — tap to reveal
Germany sent a secret message to Mexico, asking Mexico to attack the US in exchange for help getting Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona back. The British intercepted it and showed it to the US — Americans were furious!
⚓ Unrestricted Submarine Warfare — tap to reveal
Germany declared it would sink ANY ship near Britain — including American ships. This was a direct threat to US trade and lives. Germany was basically daring the US to fight.
🗽 "Make the World Safe for Democracy" — tap to reveal
President Wilson asked Congress to declare war on April 6, 1917. He said the US was fighting to protect democracy and freedom worldwide. Congress agreed, and the US officially entered the war!
🌊🚢💥

The Lusitania — A Turning Point

On May 7, 1915, a German submarine (called a U-boat) fired a torpedo at the British ocean liner Lusitania off the coast of Ireland. The massive ship sank in just 18 minutes. Many Americans who had wanted to stay neutral began to change their minds after this tragedy.

📅 Key Date: April 6, 1917

This is the day the United States officially declared war on Germany and joined the Allied Powers. American troops — called the American Expeditionary Forces (AEF) — were led by General John "Black Jack" Pershing. Thousands of Minnesotans would soon be heading to France!

🏕️ Camp Dodge & Fort Snelling

Many Minnesotans trained at Fort Snelling in St. Paul before heading overseas. Camp Dodge in Iowa also trained thousands of men from Minnesota. The 34th Infantry Division — nicknamed the "Sandstorm Division" — included many Minnesota soldiers.

🔥 Iron Range & Industry

Minnesota's Iron Range (places like Hibbing, Virginia, and Eveleth) was critically important to the war. Iron ore from Minnesota was turned into steel for weapons, ships, tanks, and railroad tracks. Without Minnesota's iron mines, the war effort would have struggled greatly!

🌾 Food Will Win the War!

Minnesota farms were vital. The US Food Administration, led by Herbert Hoover, asked Americans to have "Wheatless Wednesdays" and "Meatless Mondays" to save food for troops. Minnesota farmers grew huge amounts of wheat, corn, and other crops to feed soldiers in Europe.

Notable Minnesotans in WWI:
👨‍✈️

Governor Joseph Burnquist

Burnquist led Minnesota's aggressive war support effort. He ran the Minnesota Commission of Public Safety (MCPS), which organized civilian support, tracked war production, and unfortunately also suppressed the rights of German-Americans in Minnesota.

Minnesota Governor 1915–1921
✊

Non-Partisan League Farmers

Not all Minnesotans supported the war eagerly. Many farmers — especially from Scandinavian backgrounds — questioned why they should fight in Europe's war. Some faced harassment for their views. The war created tension between patriotism and free speech in Minnesota.

Free speech debate in wartime
👩

Minnesota Women in the War

Women couldn't fight but they contributed enormously. They worked in factories, rolled bandages for the Red Cross, raised funds through Liberty Bonds, and kept farms running while men were away. Some women served as nurses overseas in France.

Home Front Heroes

😟 The Dark Side: Anti-German Prejudice

Minnesota had a large German-American population — many families had immigrated from Germany in the 1800s. During WWI, these citizens faced unfair suspicion and discrimination. German-language classes were dropped from schools, German street names were changed, and some German-Americans were called disloyal. This is an important reminder that fear can lead to injustice.

How did the war end?
The 11th hour of the 11th day of the 11th month...
🕯️🕊️🤝

November 11, 1918 — The Armistice

At exactly 11:00 AM on November 11, 1918, guns across Europe fell silent. An armistice (agreement to stop fighting) was signed between the Allied Powers and Germany. After more than 4 years of devastating war, it was finally over. Crowds in cities around the world — including Minneapolis and St. Paul — celebrated in the streets!

The Path to Peace — key events:
Spring 1918

Germany's Last Offensive

Germany launched a massive attack hoping to win before American troops fully arrived. It almost worked — but the Allies held on.

Summer 1918

Americans Arrive in Force

Over a million fresh American soldiers poured into France, giving the Allies new strength and hope. The tide turned against Germany.

September–October 1918

The Hundred Days Offensive

The Allies launched a huge counterattack. Germany and its allies began retreating and collapsing. Austria-Hungary fell apart; Germany's Kaiser Wilhelm II fled.

November 11, 1918

The Armistice — War Ends!

Germany signed the armistice at 5 AM, and fighting stopped at 11 AM. WWI was over. Minnesotans celebrated from Duluth to Mankato!

June 28, 1919

Treaty of Versailles

The official peace treaty was signed in France. Germany was blamed for the war and forced to pay huge fines (called reparations), give up land, and shrink its military. Many historians believe these harsh terms helped lead to World War II.

📋 Wilson's 14 Points

President Wilson had a plan for lasting peace — his "14 Points." Key ideas included: freedom of the seas, reducing armies, letting people choose their own governments (self-determination), and — most importantly — creating a League of Nations where countries could solve problems without fighting. Sadly, the US Senate refused to join the League, weakening it.

🏠 Minnesotans Come Home

Returning soldiers were welcomed with parades in Minneapolis, St. Paul, Duluth, and smaller towns across the state. But many soldiers struggled with wounds — both physical and mental. WWI was the first war where "shell shock" (now called PTSD) was recognized. Minnesota communities worked hard to welcome veterans home and help them rebuild their lives.

⚠️ Seeds of the Next War

The harsh treatment of Germany in the Treaty of Versailles caused economic collapse and resentment in Germany. This created conditions that allowed Adolf Hitler to rise to power — setting the stage for World War II just 20 years later. WWI's "peace" didn't last.
